
**Wind** is the dominant dynamic driver. Speed determines rate of spread almost linearly in most fuel types; direction determines where. Critically, _local_ wind matters more than synoptic — terrain-channeled winds, slope-valley circulations, and fire-induced winds (the fire modifying its own environment) create the gaps between forecast and reality. The hardest prediction problems involve wind shifts.

**Fuel moisture content**, especially fine dead fuel moisture (1-hr and 10-hr timelag classes). This is the gatekeeping variable — it determines whether fire spreads at all and how intensely. Live fuel moisture matters for crown fire initiation. FMC integrates temperature, humidity, and precipitation history into a single operationally critical number. Most fire behavior models (Rothermel and descendants) are highly sensitive to it.

**Topography** — slope steepness, aspect, and terrain features. Slope preheats fuel above the fire through radiation and convection, roughly doubling rate of spread for every 20° of slope in some models. Canyons and chimneys create dangerous alignment effects. Topography is the one factor that's static and fully knowable in advance, which makes it the most reliable predictor component.
