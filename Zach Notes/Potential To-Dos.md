Your list covers the technical build and the business case. What's missing is the material that makes judges actually remember you.

**Demo scenario design.** Someone should be building the specific fire scenario you'll demonstrate — not just "it works on a grid" but a named, real location with real terrain. Pull LANDFIRE for a section of the 2020 Creek Fire or 2018 Camp Fire. Show the RAWS stations that were actually there (and how far away they were). Run IGNIS on the historical conditions. Show where the system would have sent drones and what it would have learned. "If this system existed during the Camp Fire, it would have detected the crown fire transition risk in Concow 45 minutes before the blowup" is a pitch-winning sentence. This requires someone spending a day on scenario selection, data preparation, and narrative construction — not code.

**Validation methodology.** How would you actually prove this works beyond simulation? Someone should draft a concrete validation plan: partner with a prescribed burn program (USFS conducts hundreds annually), deploy 3-5 drones with multispectral cameras, run IGNIS in parallel with standard monitoring, compare prediction accuracy. This shows judges you've thought past the hackathon. Include a rough budget and timeline — prescribed burn access is free through USFS research partnerships, drone hardware is ~$50-100K, compute is negligible.

**Regulatory and operational pathway.** The FAA/NASA UTM integration is actively happening. Someone should map the specific regulatory steps: Part 107 waiver for BVLOS operations near fire, the new FAA Wildland Fire UAS policy (2023), how IGNIS integrates with existing Incident Command System (ICS) structure. Which ICS role would operate it? Probably the Air Tactical Group Supervisor or the newly created UAS coordinator role. This shows operational realism.

**IP and prior art summary.** A one-page document showing: Nakano & Uno (2024) did QUBO for static sensor placement. Mandel's group did EnKF for wildfire. Krause did submodular sensor placement. IPP community did adaptive drone routing. Nobody combined them. This is your freedom-to-operate argument and your novelty claim in one page.

**Cost-benefit analysis.** How much does a wildfire cost vs. how much does earlier, better prediction save? The 2018 Camp Fire caused $16.5 billion in damage. If better prediction enables 30 minutes earlier evacuation of Paradise, how many lives and structures does that save? A 5-drone IGNIS deployment costs ~$100K in hardware plus operational costs. The break-even is absurdly favorable. One slide with these numbers.

**Technical risk matrix for judges.** Judges will ask "what could go wrong?" Have a prepared, honest answer: the information gain differential might be small in homogeneous terrain (we test this), loop closure time might be too slow for extreme fires (we scope to moderate fires), drone measurements degrade near fire (we measure ahead of the fire, not at it). Prepared honesty about risks is more persuasive than claiming none exist.

**One-page system summary.** Not the technical docs — a single page with the architecture diagram, the observation gap numbers (zero in-situ measurements within fire perimeters, 50 km RAWS spacing, drones provide 20-25 targeted measurements per hour), and the three-sentence value proposition. This is what judges take away and discuss during deliberation. If it's not on one page, it doesn't exist for scoring purposes.
